What "ready" actually means

Most clients underestimate the gap between "design is done" and "the site is live." A real launch checklist looks like:

  • Final design approved across all breakpoints (desktop, tablet, mobile)
  • All copy proofread by the client
  • All real images and brand assets in place (no Lorem Ipsum, no placeholder photos)
  • Forms tested with real submissions hitting real endpoints
  • Cross-browser tested (Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge)
  • Analytics installed and verified
  • SEO meta, sitemap, robots.txt, OG cards, schema
  • SSL, custom domain, redirects configured
  • Backup, monitoring, error tracking configured
  • Client trained on how to update content

Each item adds days. Skipping them adds weeks of post-launch firefighting.

What actually slows projects down

Client-side delays (the #1 cause)

Most projects don't slow because of code. They slow because the client takes 6 days to send back feedback on a design proposal. A 4-week project becomes a 10-week project with three rounds of "I'll review this over the weekend." If you want fast delivery, commit to 24–48 hour feedback turnaround.

Scope creep

"Can we also add..." kills more timelines than any technical issue. Healthy project management means writing scope down, treating new requests as change orders, and being explicit about timeline impact.

Third-party integrations

"Just hook this up to our CRM" can be 2 hours or 2 weeks depending on whether the CRM has a real API, sandbox, and clear docs. Always pad estimates for integrations.

Content debt

If real copy and images aren't ready, design has to start from placeholders. Then you redesign when real content arrives because the placeholder lengths were wrong. Have content ready first.

The phases

For any non-trivial project I run four phases:

  1. Discovery (week 1) — scoping, technical decisions, design direction.
  2. Design (weeks 2–3) — wireframes then high-fidelity mockups for at least the home and one inner page.
  3. Build (weeks 3–N) — staged on a private URL the client can review weekly. New routes show up incrementally.
  4. Launch & support (final week + 30 days) — production deploy, content migration, training, then a month of post-launch fixes baked in.

For tiny landing pages I collapse design and build into one phase. For SaaS work I add a "validation" phase before discovery where we sketch the smallest possible v1 that proves the idea.

How to actually get a fast delivery

  • Have content ready before you start. Copy first, design second.
  • Trust your developer's design judgment. Three opinionated rounds beat seven indecisive ones.
  • Cut scope, not corners. Ship 60% of features that are 100% solid, not 100% of features that are 60% solid.
  • Reply within 24 hours during build. Every late reply slows the next milestone by the same amount.
